On November 11, 1985, the M-19 guerrilla laid siege to the Palace of Justice. They took everyone who was inside the building as hostages: magistrates, State councillors, occasional visitors, service staff. In a manner of minutes, the army and the police surrounded the building.
The scene was like a battlefield. The confrontation between the police, the army and the guerrilla fighters would last a whole day.
Around 5 p.m., President Betancur and his ministers, who were gathered, managed to communicate with M-19. They informed them that they were completely surrounded. If they surrendered, they promised to respect their life and submit them to a fair trial. The guerrilla fighters answered that M-19 never surrendered, but they accepted a dialogue, if the army and police stopped shooting. The response from the government was yes, but on the condition that they ceased fire first, and freed the hostages.
Ultimately, they never agreed who would cease fire first.
The television networks complied with an order and stopped reporting live. They would only report at night what had happened and resumed regular programming, which included a soccer match from a local tournament.
The army blew up the walls to the palace and "exterminate" the M19. TV stations were ordered to stop broadcasting the event and resume regular programming, which included a soccer match from a local tournament. The attack was relentless.
At around 9pm, Colonel Alfonso Plazas Vega descedend from a tank in the middle of Bolívar Square. Journalists ran to interview him.
"If they’re shooting at me, I’m going to answer. I went in with my vehicles, they welcomed me with fire. I fired. I don’t know, what would you do? "
[Journalist]: But the president of the Supreme Court has asked for a ceasefire…
"I don’t know. I don’t know who’s there. Who’s inside or who’s outside. I know that several magistrates got out. I don’t know if maybe he’s among them."
"To preserve democracy, maestro.", states Plazas to a vague question about their directive or aim.
The last news the people got was around 10pm: The eastern part of the Palace of Justice is in flames. It is practically destroyed. Some people, including secretaries and magistrates, are jumping over the edge from the windows. There is total confusion right now in the building where the highest courts function: the Supreme Court and the State Council.
With the Palace of Justice in flames, the troops retreated. At about 2 a.m., when the flames had gone down, a tank shot a rocket at the facade of the building so the smoke could come out. There were no more shots that night.
To this date, we do not have the whole truth about what went on during that 11 of November 1985.

What should be done?
The peace treatise is meant to be the process through which we can move on. For war, nothing.
EL TESTIGO shows us people and their struggles with truth and forgiveness: we see people looking for truth and a way to forgive.
This documentary has us follow Jesus Abad Colorado as he returns to meet up with some of the people from his most iconic photographs and talk about truth and forgiveness.
The paradox of forgiveness: the only thing that is forgivable is the unforgivable
to forgive ≠ to forget
to forgive ≠ to reconcile
to forgive ≠ to understand the reasons of the others
to forgive ≠ to distance oneself emotionally
to forgive is to transform oneself
Genuine forgiveness does not forget, but does not cling. It is a change in the person, but not as a matter of a change in memory but rather a change in meaning and value.
To forgive is to change one's personal values, that which gives sense, and so become a different person. It is more like a mystical experience that yields a religious conversion than a calculus of harms and gains.
Asking someone to genuinely forgive is to ask to change in their core. It is a big ask. And one cannot change oneself through a sheer act of the will.
